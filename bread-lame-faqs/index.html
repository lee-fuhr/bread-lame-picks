Quick answer

A bread lame is a simple tool that holds a razor blade for scoring dough before baking. The best choice depends on your bread style and comfort; curved blades suit rustic loaves and batards, while straight blades handle baguettes and detailed cuts. Focus on blade quality, handle ergonomics, and safety features rather than price.

What is a bread lame?

A bread lame (pronounced “lahm”) is a small tool designed to hold a razor blade at a controlled angle for slashing the top of bread dough just before baking. Scoring allows the loaf to expand properly in the oven, creating the characteristic “ear” and controlling where the bread bursts. Most lames consist of a handle and a blade holder, often with a protective cap. They range from simple wooden sticks with a blade slot to more ergonomic metal or plastic models. The key difference from a plain razor is the blade’s angle and the ability to make consistent, shallow cuts without dragging the dough.

Straight vs curved blade: which should I choose?

Curved blades (often called “grignettes”) are the standard choice for scoring rustic boules and batards. The curve naturally creates a flap or “ear” as the dough expands, giving a classic artisan look. They’re easier for beginners because the blade follows a smooth arc without catching. Straight blades are better for baguettes, detailed patterns (like wheat stalks or crosses), and very controlled cuts. They require more precision and a shallower angle to avoid deflating the dough. Many experienced bakers own both and switch based on the loaf shape.

What blade material is best?

Standard double-edge razor blades (often called “safety razor blades”) are the most common and affordable option. They are sharp, thin, and widely available. Stainless steel blades resist rust but may dull slightly faster; carbon steel blades hold an edge longer but need more careful drying to prevent corrosion. Some specialty lames use thicker, single-edge blades or ceramic blades, but these are less common and often more expensive. For most home bakers, a pack of quality stainless double-edge blades offers the best balance of sharpness, cost, and convenience.

How do I hold and use a bread lame?

Grip the lame like a pencil or a paring knife, with the blade angled roughly 30 to 45 degrees relative to the dough surface. The cut should be swift and confident, hesitation can drag the dough. For a classic ear, slice off-center at a shallow angle, about a quarter-inch deep. Practice on proofed dough that is cold (retarded in the fridge) because firm dough holds its shape better. Dust the top with flour or rice flour before scoring to create contrast. If the blade sticks, it’s either too deep or the dough is too wet; adjust your angle or chill the dough longer.

How do I clean and maintain a bread lame?

After each use, rinse the blade with hot water (no soap, soap can leave residue that may affect gluten) and dry thoroughly to prevent rust. Carbon steel blades are especially prone to spotting if left damp. A quick spray of food-safe mineral oil on the blade and hinge (if applicable) can prolong sharpness. Replace the blade every few bakes, or as soon as it feels dull. A fresh blade makes cleaner cuts and reduces tearing. The handle itself can be wiped with a damp cloth; avoid soaking wood handles. Store the lame with the protective cap on or in a dry drawer to keep it safe and hygienic.

What features should I look for in a lame handle?

Comfort and control are paramount. Handles made of wood, plastic, or stainless steel are all fine, but the shape should fit your hand without slipping. Some lames are designed as a simple stick with a slot, while others have ergonomic curves or rubberized grips. A magnetic or clip-on cap is a big safety plus for storage. Also consider how easy the blade is to change: some lames use a screw or spring mechanism, others simply slide a new blade in. Avoid handles that force you to touch the bare blade during replacement.

Frequently asked questions

Can I use a bare razor blade instead of a lame?

Yes, many bakers start with a double-edge blade held between thumb and forefinger. The trade-off is less control and higher risk of nicks. A lame provides a better grip, a safer blade angle, and often a protective cover.

How deep should I score bread dough?

Aim for about a quarter to a half inch deep, depending on dough hydration and your desired ear. Deeper cuts produce a more dramatic expansion but can deflate a very wet dough. Start shallow and experiment.

Do I need a different lame for sourdough vs yeasted bread?

No, the same lame works for both. Sourdough often benefits from a curved blade because of its stiff, hydrated structure, but a straight blade can also be used. The key difference is timing: score sourdough just before baking, while yeasted doughs may be scored after proofing.

Why does my dough stick to the blade when scoring?

Sticking usually means the blade is dull, the dough is too warm or wet, or your angle is too flat. Chill the shaped dough in the fridge for 15–30 minutes, dust with flour, and use a fresh, sharp blade with a quick, fluid motion.

What is the best way to store a bread lame?

Store it with the protective cap on, in a dry spot away from moisture and heat. If you have multiple blades, keep them in their original paper wrapper or a small tin to prevent dulling. Never leave a bare blade loose in a drawer.

Are there lames specifically for left-handed bakers?

Most lames are ambidextrous, but the blade angle can be flipped. Some curved-blade lames feature a bevel that favors a dominant hand; check product descriptions. For left-handed users, a straight blade is neutral, and a curved lame can be rotated 180° to work in the opposite direction.

In shortChoosing a bread lame comes down to your preferred loaf shape, how comfortable the handle feels, and your budget. A simple curved lame with replaceable double-edge blades will serve most home bakers well, focus on building a confident scoring technique rather than chasing expensive upgrades. Happy baking!
